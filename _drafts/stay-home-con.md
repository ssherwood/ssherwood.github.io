I recommend that developers go to at least one technical conference every other year (even more
frequently if possible).  The actual event itself doesn't matter as much as the opportunity to
escape the confines of the local office to socialize with developers outside your immediate
social circles as well as the ability to attend technical sessions that can inspire new ideas or
introduce new tools or techniques to your intellectual toolbox.

However, in some years, budgets are tight; in others, deadlines loom.  Even when things seem to be
going well, life can happen.  For whatever the reason, if you find yourself not being able to attend
one of the many excellent technical conferences available, be heartened because you still can get
access to some great technical content online if you know where to look.

Additionally, if you do a little up front negotiation and pre-planing, you can create a sort of
"virtual conference" for yourself that you can enjoy at home.

# The Negotiation

First, negotiate with your manager to take a few days off to "work from home".  Make it clear to
them that you intend to spend the time attending virtual technical sessions in lieu of a full
conference.  Point out that you will be saving the company money by not having to pay for travel,
food and expensive hotels.  Also make it clear that, like a conference, you won't be able to respond
as frequently to emails or other queries due to being "off site".

It is also important to set expectations with your family that you will be working during this time
and should not be disturbed (unless it is an emergency, of course).  Close the door to your home
office and ask that family members text or email you if they need something.  Insist to being
treated as if you really are away at an actual conference.  You need un-interrupted time to watch
and digest the sessions that you've chosen, not unlike the real thing.

Ultimately, make sure that all of the parties involved can agree to your terms.

# Planing Your Event

Find a future date where your work calendar is clear and book the time as "out of office" (its okay
if you have to cancel meeting or two).  Make sure to reserve at least 2 contiguous days for your
virtual conference and set a daily schedule that mirrors real technical conferences with multiple
sessions and breaks.  Don't be afraid to put in extra time at the beginning and endo of the day to
do a deep dive into any interesting topics that you encounter.

Next, sit down and browse YouTube and/or the links provided and build your itinerary.  Pick videos
that have topics that will interest or challenge you -- and don't be afraid to pick a few topics way
outside of your comfort zone.
  
Finally, put them on your calendar and let your boss know.
